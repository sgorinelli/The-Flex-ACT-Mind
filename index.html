The Flex Mind

A Deep-Dive 5-Week Acceptance & Commitment Therapy Experience

Welcome to Psychological Flexibility

Have you ever felt like you are fighting a war inside your own mind? Trying to forcefully "think positive," eliminate anxiety, or stop feeling sad? It takes a massive amount of energy, doesn't it?

Core Concept: Creative Hopelessness

Before we begin, we need to look honestly at what you've already tried. Most of us try to control our internal world (thoughts and feelings) the same way we control the outside world. If there's a rock in your shoe, you take it out. So naturally, if there's sadness in your mind, you try to pull it out.

But ask yourself: Has running away from, suppressing, or fighting your difficult emotions permanently worked? If you are completely honest, fighting your mind probably hasn't brought lasting peace. Realizing that your old strategies are "hopeless" isn't depressing—it's liberating. It means you can finally drop the heavy armor and try something radically new.

Most approaches to mental health try to reduce symptoms. Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) takes a different approach.

"The goal of ACT is to create a rich and meaningful life, while accepting the pain that inevitably goes with it." — Dr. Russ Harris

Core Concept: Workability

In this program, we are not going to ask if a thought is "true" or "false." We are going to ask if it is Workable. Does listening to this thought help you build the life you want, or does it keep you stuck? Workability is our ultimate measuring stick.

Over the next five weeks, we will train your mind to step back from unhelpful thoughts, ground yourself in the present, and most importantly, take action toward what you actually care about.

Week 1: Discovering Your Compass

Before we can navigate the storms of the mind, we need to know where we are sailing. In ACT, we differentiate heavily between Goals, Rules, and Values.

A Goal is a destination. "Losing 10 pounds," or "Getting a promotion." Once achieved, it's checked off. If you fail, you feel worthless; if you succeed, you ask, "Is that all there is?"

A Rule is a rigid standard. "I must never show weakness." Rules are brittle; when they break, we crash.

A Value is a direction on a compass. For example, "Being loving" or "Being industrious." You can never "finish" being loving. Values are always available to you, right in this exact moment, regardless of circumstances.

Week 2: Taking Committed Action

Last week, we calibrated your compass. This week, we start walking. Committed Action means taking effective steps guided by your values, even when it brings up psychological discomfort.

Core Concept: The Choice Point

Imagine your life as a constant series of forks in the road. In every moment, a situation or internal feeling triggers a choice point.

Trigger: Feeling Anxious / Overwhelmed

AWAY MOVES

←

Actions taken to avoid difficult feelings.
(e.g., Canceling plans, doomscrolling, snapping at partner).

Short term: Relief. Long term: Stuck.

TOWARDS MOVES

→

Actions guided by values, carrying the difficult feelings with you.
(e.g., Going to the event anyway, speaking up).

Short term: Discomfort. Long term: Meaning.

The Passengers on the Bus Metaphor

Imagine you are driving a bus toward your Values. Along the way, you pick up unruly passengers: anxiety, self-doubt, and harsh self-judgment.

When you make a "Towards" move, these passengers rush to the front and yell, "You're going to crash! You're a fraud!" To quiet them, you might veer off course (an "Away" move) just to keep them happy in the back of the bus.

Committed action is realizing you are the driver. They can yell, but they cannot turn the steering wheel.

Exercise 2: The Quick 3-Zone Body Scan

We often carry our psychological stress in our physical body without noticing. Click through the zones below to do a rapid, active grounding scan.

Focus on your face.

Are your teeth clenched? Is your tongue pressed against the roof of your mouth? Are you squinting your eyes slightly? Action: Let your lower jaw drop slightly. Soften the space between your eyebrows.

Focus on your neck and shoulders.

Are your shoulders creeping up toward your ears in a defensive posture? Action: On your next exhale, actively drop your shoulders down an inch. Imagine the tension draining down your arms and out your fingertips.

Focus on your abdomen.

Are you sucking your stomach in or breathing very shallowly in your chest? Action: Let your belly go completely soft. Take one breath so deep that only your belly expands, not your chest.

Week 4: Untangling from Thoughts

The human mind is a problem-solving machine designed to keep us alive. It evolved to constantly look for danger. But in the modern world, it treats thoughts like "I might fail this interview" with the exact same physiological alarm as "There is a tiger outside."

Cognitive Fusion (Being Hooked) is when we are tangled up in our thoughts. We believe they are absolute truths, commands we must obey, or realities we are trapped in. We view the world from our thoughts.

Cognitive Defusion (Unhooking) is stepping back. It’s looking at thoughts rather than looking from them. We recognize that thoughts are just words, sounds, and pictures popping up in our heads.

Core Concept: Perspective-Taking (The Observer Self)

If you are not your thoughts, who are you? You are the observer of the thoughts. Think of a chessboard. The white pieces are your positive thoughts. The black pieces are your negative thoughts. Most of our lives, we fight on the board, trying to make the white pieces defeat the black pieces.

But you are not the pieces. You are the board. The board doesn't care who wins; it simply provides the space for the game to happen. You are the space where your thoughts occur.

Exercise 1: Unhooking Playground

Unhooking often feels playful, sometimes even silly. By changing the context of a thought, we strip away its emotional power without trying to change its content. Click the flashcards below.

1. Name the Story

Our minds tell the same stories repeatedly. "Ah, here goes the 'I'm an Imposter' story again." Acknowledge it like an old movie.

2. "I am having the thought..."

Instead of "I am a failure", say: "I notice I am having the thought that I am a failure."

3. The Silly Voice

Take a harsh thought and say it in a cartoon voice. Notice how it loses its threat level.

4. Sing it to "Happy Birthday"

Take your painful thought ("Nobody likes me") and mentally sing it to the tune of Happy Birthday. It breaks the illusion of a serious threat.

5. Thank Your Mind

When your mind gives you a terrible worst-case scenario, say, "Thank you, mind! I know you're just trying to protect me, but I've got this."

6. The Computer Screen

Visualize your negative thought written on a screen. Imagine changing the font to Comic Sans, changing the color to neon pink, and shrinking it.

Exercise 2: The Chessboard Editor

To prove that thoughts are just constructed objects (not reality), let's manipulate them. Enter a positive thought and a negative thought, then use the controls to alter their size, font, and opacity.

Week 5: Acceptance & Self-Compassion

Core Concept: Experiential Avoidance

Much of our suffering comes from Experiential Avoidance—our desperate attempts to push away sadness, anxiety, or grief. We use alcohol, endless scrolling, isolation, or overworking to avoid feeling bad. But running from pain just creates a second layer of suffering: the suffering of the struggle itself. Acceptance is the exact opposite of avoidance. It is turning toward the pain.

The Struggle Switch

Dr. Russ Harris uses the metaphor of the Struggle Switch to explain Clean vs. Dirty pain. Interact with the switch below.

SWITCH IS ON: FIGHTING THE FEELING
Anxiety shows up (Clean Pain). You fight it. It amplifies into panic (Dirty Pain). You are trapped.

The Willingness Dial

Once you turn the struggle switch off, you can begin to turn the Willingness Dial up. Willingness means: "How open am I to experiencing this feeling right now, in order to live my values?"

Exercise 1: Physicalizing the Emotion

Instead of fighting a feeling, try studying it like a curious scientist. Think of something mildly stressful right now, and locate the physical feeling in your body.

Now, imagine your breath flowing directly into and around that object. You aren't trying to blow it away; you are just making a comfortable space in your body for it to exist. Let it be there.

Exercise 2: The Self-Compassion Double Standard

Doing this work is incredibly courageous. Yet, we often treat ourselves with a harshness we would never inflict on someone else. Before listening to the final audio, try this reflection.

A Friend Fails

Imagine a close friend you love comes to you in tears. They failed at something important or made a huge mistake. What tone of voice do you use? What exact words do you say to them?

You Fail

Now, imagine you make that exact same mistake. What is the tone of your internal voice? What exact words does your mind immediately say to you?

Core Concept: Common Humanity

Dr. Kristin Neff teaches that self-compassion requires recognizing Common Humanity. When we fail or hurt, our mind tells us: "You are the only one struggling with this. Everyone else has it together." This is a lie. Suffering, making mistakes, and feeling inadequate is the shared human experience. You are not alone in this feeling; it connects you to the rest of humanity.
